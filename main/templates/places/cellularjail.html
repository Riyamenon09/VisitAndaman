Cellular Jail — National Memorial

Overview

The Cellular Jail, also known as Kala Pani, is the most powerful symbol of India’s freedom struggle in the Andaman & Nicobar Islands. Built by the British between 1896–1906, its seven spokes of solitary cells once housed political prisoners including Veer Savarkar and Batukeshwar Dutt. Today, it’s preserved as a National Memorial with galleries that narrate the story of sacrifice and resilience.

  • Location: Atlanta Point, Port Blair
  • Time needed: 1.5–2 hours (museum + viewpoint)
  • Best time: Oct–Apr, visit around 3–5 PM and stay for the light & sound show

What to See

  • Solitary Cells: Rows of individual cells designed to enforce isolation.
  • Central Tower: The hub from which the seven wings radiated (three stand today).
  • Freedom Fighters’ Gallery: Exhibits, photos, and artifacts from the independence movement.
  • Rooftop Viewpoint: Panoramic views of Port Blair and the sea.

Light & Sound Show

After sunset, the walls come alive with narration, music, and projection that recount the jail’s history and the lives of the freedom fighters. Language and schedule vary by season; tickets sell out on busy days, so book earlier in the afternoon at the counter or via local operators.

  • Typical timings: 6:00–7:00 PM & 7:30–8:30 PM (check locally for the day’s schedule)
  • Language: Usually Hindi & English shows on different slots
  • Arrive 15–20 minutes early for good seats

Getting There

Cellular Jail is in the heart of Port Blair (Atlanta Point), about 10–15 minutes from Veer Savarkar International Airport (IXZ). Auto-rickshaws are the easiest option; parking is available near the entrance. You can club the visit with the nearby Water Sports Complex and a sunset stop at Corbyn’s Cove.

Tips

  • Carry water; summers are humid. Hats/umbrellas help on the rooftop.
  • Photography is allowed in most areas; follow signage for restricted spots.
  • If you’re short on time, do museum → corridors → rooftop → show.
